Cloud Services

What does it do?

The cloud. An amazing introduction to the everyday use of technology and what a significant impact it has had. Gone the days of having hard drives in every corner of one's desk, or a pencil case full of USB flash drives, everything is now in the cloud. Many wonder what this mysterious cloud really is. According to Microsoft; The definition for the cloud can seem murky, but essentially, it’s a term used to describe a global network of servers, each with a unique function. The cloud is not a physical entity, but instead is a vast network of remote servers around the globe which are hooked together and meant to operate as a single ecosystem. These servers are designed to either store and manage data, run applications, or deliver content or a service such as streaming videos, web mail, office productivity software, or social media. Instead of accessing files and data from a local or personal computer, you are accessing them online from any Internet-capable device—the information will be available anywhere you go and anytime you need it. It may seem that the same technology is essentially still being used, since these services are still being hosted from a physical device (or server), just not on premises, rather in a datacentre(s). This has had an enormous impact not only on individuals but also for enterprise use. Companies are now able to outsource a lot of their work and not have to worry about regular maintenance and other work that is involved in troubleshooting these resources. An example of this would be migrating locally storage files to either Microsoft OneDrive or Google Drive, which is also stored in the cloud (I.e., in Microsoft and Googles Datacentres). Another example would be having a less reliant on an on-premises Domain Controller and domain joining their devices to what is called the Active Directory, Users and Computers. This can now be done of premises in what is called Microsoft Azure, or Azure Active Directory, all through the cloud. As can be seen from the above, there are a variety of services inherent with cloud computing. These mainly revolve around Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS), Software as a Service (SaaS), and finally Platform as a Service (PaaS). There are a variety of factors that have pushed for the need and increase demand for cloud computing. These include but are not limited to economic benefits. This is referring to the cost savings for companies on hardware, but also the costs saved on wages and workers. Regular maintenance of on premises servers in now no longer required as cloud-based servers are stored and managed through a datacentre. Another enormous driving factor is security. Many firms struggle to properly implement means of security for their infrastructure, due to many factors, however, cloud-based services have full time security teams managing their clients' services.

What is the likely impact?

The idea and invention of cloud computing and the services in which it provides has had an enormous impact upon our world. Bringing countless scenarios of efficiency, speed, cost savings, advanced security and many more, and it only appears to be getting better, with the demand for cloud services on the rise. With consumers increasing their expectation for efficiency and other needs from cloud services, datacentres are appearing in areas quicker than ever. They are soon to be able to fit more servers in a smaller area than ever before thanks to the advancements in technological hardware. At the end of the day, the “cloud” so to speak, will always require a physical hardware to be hosted upon. As a result of this invention, and as its popularity increases, companies will require less maintenance on their onsite servers, as they become migrated to datacentres. There are a variety of people that will mostly be affected include personal consumers. This is because people can now completely rely on cloud storage rather than physical hard drives. The enterprise and education sector will also vastly be affected by cloud services in a positive way due to its amazing collaboration tools, storage, and many other services in which it provides. This may result in the loss of jobs for onsite desktop and server technicians, although these people may find themselves doing the same work on a much grander scale within a datacentre, rather than a small company.
